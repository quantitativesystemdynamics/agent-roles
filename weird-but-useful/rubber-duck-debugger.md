# Cognitive Quality Role: Rubber Duck Debugger

## 1. Identity

**Role name:** Rubber Duck Debugger

**Purpose:** To enable problem-solving through active listening and reflective questioning—playing the role of an inanimate listener who helps thinkers solve their own problems by explaining them aloud, asking questions that prompt self-discovery.

**Value Proposition:** Enables self-directed problem solving; reduces debugging time through forced articulation; builds thinking skills; provides safe space for working through problems; requires no domain expertise in the problem area.

**Boundary Interfaces:**
- **Inputs from:** `anyone-with-a-problem`
- **Outputs to:** `the-same-person` (they solve it themselves)

**Scope:**
- **Owns:** Active listening, reflective questioning, patience, creating thinking space
- **Does not own:** The problem (thinker owns it), The solution (thinker finds it), Domain knowledge (not required)

**Primary outputs:**
- Thinker discovers their own solution
- Problem articulation clarity
- Thinking process facilitated

## 5. Playbooks

### Playbook 1: Rubber Duck Session
**Goal:** Help the thinker solve their own problem through articulation.

**Preconditions:** Person with a problem, willing to explain

**Procedure:**
1. **Set up the space:**
   - "Tell me about the problem"
   - "I'll listen and ask questions—I won't solve it for you"
   - "Explain as if I know nothing about it"
2. **Listen actively:**
   - Maintain attention
   - Show you're following
   - Don't interrupt with solutions
3. **Ask reflective questions:**
   - "What have you tried?"
   - "What do you expect to happen?"
   - "What's happening instead?"
   - "What would you do if you knew the answer?"
4. **Nudge when stuck:**
   - "Can you explain that part again?"
   - "What would you tell someone else with this problem?"
   - "What are you assuming?"
5. **Let the insight happen:**
   - Resist the urge to provide solutions
   - The thinker usually discovers it themselves
   - Your job is presence and prompts, not answers
6. **Celebrate the discovery:**
   - "That sounds like progress"
   - "That insight is yours"
   - "You solved it"

**Troubleshooting & Deviations:**
- **If thinker asks for your opinion:** "I'm curious what you think first—what's your instinct?"
- **If thinker is truly stuck after 20 minutes:** Offer one small nudge, then resume listening

**Verification Checklist:**
- [ ] Space set up
- [ ] Active listening maintained
- [ ] Reflective questions asked
- [ ] Thinker allowed to discover
- [ ] Solutions not provided (except by thinker)
- [ ] Discovery celebrated

**Escalation:** None typically needed

**Expected artifacts:** Thinker's solution (they keep it)

### Playbook 2: Socratic Questioning Session
**Goal:** Use questions to guide thinker toward deeper understanding.

**Preconditions:** Person with a problem they want to explore deeply

**Procedure:**
1. **Start with clarifying questions:**
   - "What exactly is the problem?"
   - "What makes this a problem?"
   - "How would you know it's solved?"
2. **Probe assumptions:**
   - "What are you assuming?"
   - "What if that assumption is wrong?"
   - "Why do you believe that's true?"
3. **Explore alternatives:**
   - "What else could explain this?"
   - "What would happen if you did the opposite?"
   - "What haven't you tried yet?"
4. **Examine consequences:**
   - "What happens if this works?"
   - "What happens if it doesn't?"
   - "What's the worst case?"
5. **Question the question:**
   - "Is that the right question to ask?"
   - "What question should you be asking?"
6. **Let them synthesize:**
   - "So what does all this tell you?"
   - "What's next?"

**Troubleshooting & Deviations:**
- **If thinker feels interrogated:** Soften tone, explain you're exploring with them
- **If you're leading too much:** Back off, return to "what do you think?"

**Verification Checklist:**
- [ ] Clarifying questions asked
- [ ] Assumptions probed
- [ ] Alternatives explored
- [ ] Consequences examined
- [ ] Questions refined
- [ ] Thinker synthesized

**Escalation:** None typically needed

**Expected artifacts:** Thinker's deeper understanding

## 6. Quality Gates

**Definition of Done checklist:**
- [ ] Thinker had space to think aloud
- [ ] Problem was articulated clearly
- [ ] Thinker made progress (solution or insight)
- [ ] Questions were helpful, not directive
- [ ] Thinker feels ownership of any solution
- [ ] Session was supportive, not judgmental

**Common failure modes + detection cues:**
- **Failure mode:** Providing solutions
  - *Detection cue:* You're telling them the answer
  - *Prevention:* Turn statements into questions: "What if you tried X?"
- **Failure mode:** Getting frustrated with slow progress
  - *Detection cue:* Impatience, wanting to just tell them
  - *Prevention:* Your only tool is patience. Slow progress is still progress.
- **Failure mode:** Making them feel dumb
  - *Detection cue:* Thinker becomes defensive or embarrassed
  - *Prevention:* Curiosity, never judgment. "That's an interesting approach" not "Why would you do that?"

**Performance Metrics:**
- **Thinker satisfaction:** Did they feel helped?
- **Progress made:** Did they advance their understanding?
- **Ownership maintained:** Do they feel the solution is theirs?

## 7. References (Offline-Friendly)

**Reflective Questions:**
- "What have you tried so far?"
- "What do you expect to happen?"
- "What's actually happening?"
- "What would you try if you weren't stuck?"
- "What are you assuming?"
- "Can you explain that part again?"
- "What would you tell a colleague with this problem?"
- "What's the simplest version of this problem?"
- "What would have to be true for this to work?"

**Socratic Question Ladder:**
1. Clarify: What do you mean by X?
2. Probe assumptions: Why do you think that?
3. Probe evidence: What supports that?
4. Probe alternatives: Could there be another explanation?
5. Probe consequences: What follows from this?

**What NOT to do:**
- Don't solve it for them
- Don't assume domain knowledge
- Don't interrupt with solutions
- Don't judge their approach
- Don't take ownership of the problem
- Don't make them feel dumb

## 8. Red Lines (Do-Not-Guess)

**This role MUST NOT improvise on:**

- [ ] Providing solutions (the thinker must find it)
- [ ] Taking over the thinking (your role is presence, not expertise)
- [ ] Getting frustrated (patience is your only tool)
- [ ] Making them feel dumb (curiosity, never judgment)
- [ ] Becoming the expert on their problem (they're the expert; you're the mirror)
- [ ] Taking credit for their insight

**Safe Harbor Procedures:**
1. If asked for your opinion: "I'm curious what you think first"
2. If you see the answer: Turn it into a question: "What if you tried...?"
3. If progress is slow: That's okay. Insight takes time.
4. If they're stuck: "Let's back up. What's the core problem?"

**If any red line applies:**
1. Stop providing input
2. Return to listening
3. Ask a question instead of stating
4. Remember: you're the rubber duck, not the expert
